Water for dogs on alpine trails: how much, and where it actually is

Your dog can't tell you it's two litres short. Here's the arithmetic, the warning signs, and how to plan a route around real fountains instead of hope.

Dogs don't sweat their heat away: they pant it away, and panting spends water. On an exposed Dolomites trail in July, a dog is running a constant evaporative loss that its owner, sipping from a bottle in the shade of a hat brim, systematically underestimates. Most heat incidents we hear about on trail aren't exotic: they're a normal dog, a normal route, and a water plan that was really just an assumption.

The arithmetic

The commonly cited veterinary baseline is roughly one ounce of water per pound of body weight per day (call it 60 ml per kilo). That's a resting figure. With sustained exercise, and especially with heat, the requirement can double to quadruple. Worked example: a 25 kg dog needs about 1.5 litres on a sofa day; on a hot, shadeless 4-hour hike, plan for something in the range of 3 to 6 litres, between what you carry and what the trail provides.

Two practical rules follow. First, offer water every 15–30 minutes of active walking: small amounts often beats a big drink rarely, and a dog that's panting hard should be offered water before it asks. Second, at altitude the air is drier and the sun harsher than the temperature suggests, so the multiplier applies even on days that feel mild to you.

Sources: American Kennel Club: dehydration in dogs; Wilderness Medical Society: canine hydration

The signs owners miss

CheckWhat you're looking for
GumsShould be wet and slick. Dry or sticky gums are an early dehydration flag.
Skin tentGently lift the skin near the shoulder blades and release. It should snap back instantly; skin that settles slowly means fluid loss.
EyesSunken eyes are a later, more serious sign.
BehaviourLethargy, slowing on flat ground, panting heavier than the pace justifies: the same early signals as heat stress, because the two arrive together.

If you see these: shade, rest, and small amounts of water: a dehydrated dog that gulps a bowl can vomit it straight back, making things worse. If signs don't resolve quickly, it's a vet situation, not a wait-and-see situation.

About streams, lakes and troughs: alpine pastures put cattle and sheep upstream of a great many watercourses, and standing water can carry parasites like giardia. A drink from a fast, cold stream is usually fine in a pinch, but "the trail crosses a stream" is not a water plan. Cattle troughs are the worst of both worlds; skip them.

Where the water actually is

This is the part we can help with specifically. DoloPaws maps 16,581 drinking-water features across the Dolomites and Savoy (village fountains, trailhead taps, springs) as a toggleable layer on our map, and every trail page lists the water points along that specific route with their kilometre marks. That turns the vague question "will there be water?" into an actual plan: know your longest dry stretch before you start, and size what you carry to cover it.

Three planning habits worth building: check the trail page's water list against the day's heat before you commit; treat the longest gap between points, not the total count, as your carrying requirement; and remember fountains can be seasonal: hikers report dry fountains on DoloPaws, and those reports appear on the trail page, attributed and dated.

What to carry

A collapsible bowl (dogs drink poorly from bottle streams, much of it ends up on the ground), more capacity than the arithmetic says on exposed routes, and for flat-faced or heavy-coated breeds, a margin on top of that: they run hotter on less.
