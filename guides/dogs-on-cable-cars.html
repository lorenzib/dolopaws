Taking your dog on cable cars and gondolas: the rules that actually apply

Muzzles, tickets, size limits, and which lifts say yes: for the Dolomites and the French Alps, with sources you can check.

A lift can turn a brutal 900 m climb into a gentle ridge walk, which matters more for a dog than for you, because your dog can't tell you at the bottom that today isn't the day. But every summer, dog owners get turned away at valley stations for the same two reasons: no muzzle in the pack, or a dog too big for a rule they didn't know existed. Here's how it actually works.

The rules that apply almost everywhere

Operators set their own policies, but across the Dolomites four rules repeat so consistently you should treat them as standard:

1. Leash on, muzzle on. Italy requires dog owners to have a muzzle with them at all times; on board a lift, dogs are generally required to actually wear it. This is the rule most first-timers get caught by: pack a muzzle your dog has practised wearing at home, not one they meet for the first time in a queue.

2. Small dogs you can carry usually ride free. Lagazuoi, for example, defines "small" precisely: within a 50×30 cm frame, carried in your arms. Guide dogs ride free everywhere regardless of size.

3. Larger dogs need a ticket. Sometimes free, sometimes a dedicated dog fare: it depends on the operator, not on any regional standard.

4. No seats, and cooperate with the attendant. Dogs travel on the floor, and if the cabin attendant asks you to muzzle up, that request is final: it exists for the other passengers, not as a judgement of your dog.

Also in the pack: your dog's EU pet passport. It's obligatory to carry it when travelling in Italy with a dog, with the anti-rabies vaccination recorded. Puppies under three months may not enter Italy at all.
Source: Val Gardena tourism: rules for dog owners

Area by area: what the operators say

Cortina d'Ampezzo – Cortina Skiworld

The most dog-generous policy we've found in the Dolomites. On the Faloria, Tofana, Averau and 5 Torri lifts, and the three Ista chairlifts (Piè-Tofana–Duca d'Aosta, Duca d'Aosta–Pomedes, Socrepes), dogs travel free of charge regardless of size: leash and muzzle required. The operator even publishes suggested dog itineraries from the lift stations.

Source: Cortina Skiworld: pet friendly lifts

Lagazuoi cable car (Passo Falzarego)

Dogs of all sizes are welcome. Small dogs (within 50×30 cm, carried in your arms) and guide dogs ride free; larger dogs need a dedicated ticket and must wear a muzzle and stay on the leash. Dogs classified as dangerous or with transmissible illnesses are refused.

Source: Lagazuoi: carriage of dogs

Val Gardena

On all cable cars and chairlifts in the valley, dogs board on payment of a ticket, with leash and muzzle. On public buses, guide dogs and small dogs travel free; others need a ticket. Note that individual municipalities can add their own rules on top.

Source: Val Gardena tourism: rules for dog owners

Chamonix & the French Alps

Local tourist information reports that dogs on a lead are accepted on most Chamonix-area lifts and cog railways (including the Montenvers train) with notable exceptions: both sections of the Aiguille du Midi cable car refuse dogs, as does the Index chairlift above Flégère. Our own map data agrees: the Plan de l'Aiguille cable car carries an explicit "no dogs" tag in OpenStreetMap. Also important in France: several nature reserves ban dogs entirely, even leashed.

Sources: Chamonix All Year: pets; OpenStreetMap lift tagging

What we can and can't tell you (honestly)

DoloPaws maps 877 lifts across the Dolomites and Savoy: which ones run in summer, where the stations are, and how they connect to dog-suitable trails. What we don't yet have is a verified dog policy for every one of them: the policies above come from operators and tourism boards that publish them clearly, and we cite every source. Where we haven't verified a lift's policy, we say so on the map instead of guessing. As we ride lifts with dogs and confirm policies, this page grows.

Two practical consequences: always check the specific lift's website or call the valley station before building a day around it, and treat any lift's policy as seasonal: summer and winter rules can differ.
